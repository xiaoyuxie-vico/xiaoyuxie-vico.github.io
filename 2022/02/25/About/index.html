About me

There is a quote (with modifications) from the TV show Westworld:

Some people choose to see the chaos and disarray in this world. I choose to see the beauty. To believe there is an order to our days, a purpose.

For me, knowledge is the beauty. Whether it’s something as “simple” as Newton’s laws of motion (F=ma) or the mass-energy equivalence equation (e=mc^2), mathematical knowledge has the power to explain and improve various aspects of our world, including nature, technology, and culture. Scientists have been using assumptions, hypotheses, and principles to derive equations and discover knowledge for centuries, laying the foundations for our modern world. But this was a process that took decades of labor and effort.

Artificial intelligence (AI) has the potential to accelerate this process of uncovering hidden patterns and knowledge. However, many AI research efforts focus solely on data-driven approaches, which can result in low interpretability, limited generalization, and reliance on large datasets. These models are prone to inaccuracy and lack methods to explain their failures.

To address these issues, AI needs to incorporate scientific insights, such as partial differential equations and physical invariance, into its algorithms. This integration of existing knowledge, rather than solely relying on data, is what I refer to as Scientific/Mechanistic Machine Learning or AI4Science.

My research in Dimensionless Learning exemplifies this new research direction. We incorporate fundamental physical invariance, such as dimensional invariance, into machine learning to discover three levels of knowledge, including dimensionless numbers at the feature level, scaling laws at the algebraic equation level, and governing equations at the differential equation level.

Moving forward, my research will focus on two directions:

  1. Physics for AI: incorporating fundamental physical knowledge into AI algorithms to improve interpretability, generalization, and small-dataset scalability;
  2. AI for physics: developing robust and flexible AI algorithms to discover hidden patterns, equations, and scientific insights from data.

Research

1. Physics for AI

1.1 Dimensionless Learning (Dimensional Analysis + Machine Learning) for scientific discovery

Dimensionless learning combines fundamental physics principles with state-of-the-art machine learning techniques to solve scientific and engineering problems. By embedding the principle of dimensional invariance into a two-level machine learning scheme, it can automatically discover dominant dimensionless numbers and governing laws (scaling laws and differential equations) from noisy and scarce measurements. Paper (Nature Communications), Code.

1.2 Dimensionally invariant Neural Network (DimensionNet) for scientific discovery

The DimensionNet reduces the dimensionality of the original input parameters by automatically discovering a smaller set of governing dimensionless numbers and transforming the high-dimensional inputs to the dimensionless set.

2. AI for physics

AI-empowered scientific discovery and digital twin in additive manufacturing

Metal additive manufacturing provides remarkable flexibility in geometry and component design, but localized heating/cooling heterogeneity leads to spatial variations of as-built mechanical properties, significantly complicating the materials design process. …

Read more

To this end, we develop a mechanistic data-driven framework integrating wavelet transforms and convolutional neural networks to predict location-dependent mechanical properties over fabricated parts based on process-induced temperature sequences, i.e., thermal histories. The framework enables multiresolution analysis and importance analysis to reveal dominant mechanistic features underlying the additive manufacturing process, such as critical temperature ranges and fundamental thermal frequencies. We systematically compare the developed approach with other machine learning methods. The results demonstrate that the developed approach achieves reasonably good predictive capability using a small amount of noisy experimental data. It provides a concrete foundation for a revolutionary methodology that predicts spatial and temporal evolution of mechanical properties leveraging domain-specific knowledge and cutting-edge machine and deep learning technologies.

  • Xie, X., Bennett, J., Saha, S., Lu, Y., Cao, J., Liu, W. K., & Gan, Z. (2021). Mechanistic data-driven prediction of as-built mechanical properties in metal additive manufacturing. Npj Computational Materials, 7(1), 1–12.
